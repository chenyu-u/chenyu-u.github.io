§1

What this is

This is a concept for a physical robotic arm I want to build. Before ordering motors and cutting material, I wanted to know whether the arm I had in mind could actually reach the parts, sort them into a bin, and close its claw the way I pictured. So I built this simulation to test all of that on screen first. You click the floor to drop a part, and the arm plans a path and runs a pick and place into the bin.

I put the code together quickly with the help of AI tools. What I spent my own time on is the part I care about as a mechanical engineer: the geometry of the arm, the joint layout, the reach, and the claw mechanism. The goal was never to write clever software. It was to check that the machine makes sense before I build it.

§2

How I came up with the dimensions

I started from the job. The arm has to pick small cubes off a desk and drop them into a bin about a hand's width away. That set the reach. I picked link lengths of 120, 90, and 55 mm for the shoulder, forearm, and wrist, which gives a total reach a bit over 300 mm once the gripper is added. That covers the desk area I want without the arm being so long that the motors can't hold it up.

The shoulder sits 40 mm above the base plate so the arm clears the surface at full fold. The claw adds a fixed 34 mm from the wrist to the point between the fingertips. I kept every number in one place at the top of the code so I could change one length, watch the reachable area update, and settle on proportions that felt buildable. These are the same dimensions I would take into CAD and then into real parts.

§3

How the arm moves

The base spins the whole arm left and right. The other three joints all work in a single flat plane, so the hard part of the math only ever deals with two numbers: how far out the target is, and how high. To point the arm at a spot, I turn the base to face it, then solve the three planar joints to reach it.

One detail that matters more than it sounds: the arm aims the fingertips at the part, not the wrist. The gripper is a real length of the arm, so if you aim the wrist instead, every grab lands short by the length of the claw. In the simulation you would not notice because the part just snaps to the tool. On a real arm it would drive the palm into the part and stall the motor. So I treat the gripper as the last segment of the arm.

§4

Why the base is a stepper, not a servo

I wanted the arm to reach all the way around, a full circle, so it can pick from one side and place on the other. A normal hobby servo only turns 180 degrees and stops, which would cut the working area in half. So the base uses a stepper motor driving a belt instead. That gives continuous rotation and fine control over the angle.

This choice created a problem I had to handle in the design. Cables cannot twist forever. A base that truly spins without limit needs a slip ring at the column, or the controller has to unwind itself now and then. It is the kind of thing that is easy to forget on paper and expensive to find out on the bench, so I am noting it now as something the physical build has to account for.

§5

The claw, and a problem I ran into

The two fingers pivot on pins and are pushed open and closed by a single actuator through a linkage. One motor, two jaws that move together. It is the kind of gripper you can build from flat plate and a few dowel pins, which is why I chose it.

The problem I ran into was that my first version of the claw did not really move. It just slid two blocks a few millimetres apart, which was both invisible on screen and not honest about how the real thing works. So I reworked it into a proper linkage where the actuator position is solved from the finger angle, the same constraint the real aluminium parts would follow. Now the fingers swing through about 55 degrees and you can actually see them open and close. It also goes stiff near the end of its travel, the same way a real linkage does, which is useful feedback when picking the link lengths.

I track the claw by the gap between the pads in millimetres, because that is what you would measure with callipers around a part. Everything else follows from that number.

§6

Making the motion honest

My first version timed each step by hand, saying a move takes so many seconds no matter how far the arm has to travel. That is backwards from how a real machine works, and it fell apart fast. A motor has a top speed, so if I ask a joint to move further than it can in the time I gave it, the simulation and the real arm stop agreeing.

I changed it so each move has a speed, not a duration, and the time comes from how far the arm actually travels. Every joint is also capped at its own top speed. If a path asks for more than the joints can deliver, the tool falls behind and a follow error reading shows how far. That is the same kind of error a real controller reports, so it keeps the simulation honest about what the hardware could do.

A move also only finishes when the arm has actually arrived and the jaws have reached their gap, not when a timer runs out. This is how a real controller works, and it is what would let the same list of moves drive the physical arm.

§7

The pick and place cycle

Each part runs through the same eight steps.

02DESCENDprecisionStraight-line drop onto the part
03GRASPholdClose jaws to part width, then hold it
04LIFTapproachRaise to transit height
05TRANSFERrapidTraverse to above the assigned bin slot
06LOWERprecisionDescend onto the slot
07RELEASEopenOpen jaws, let the part rest in the bin
08RETRACTapproachClear the bin, then return home

§8

How this connects to a real arm

Motors do not take angles in degrees. The stepper base wants step counts, the servos want pulse widths, and the claw actuator wants a command based on how far its rod moves. So each joint in the simulation carries a calibration: its range, a flag for whether the motor is mounted backwards, and a small offset for the fact that a motor horn never lines up perfectly. The panel shows these live values while the arm moves, which is exactly what you would check before powering a real arm so it does not drive into a hard stop.

The EXPORT button solves the whole cycle and downloads it as a file listing every move with its target, joint angles, claw gap, and the actual command for each motor. That file is the real handoff to an Arduino or ESP32. The idea is that the browser is the teach pendant and the exported file is the program the physical arm runs.

§9

What this does not model yet

Being clear about the gaps, because these are the things standing between a clean run here and a working arm on the bench.

  • No gravity or torque. This is the big one. A real shoulder motor holding the arm out at full reach might stall or brown out under a load this simulation carries for free. I need to budget torque before I trust any of this on hardware.
  • No grasp force. Closing to the part width assumes a rigid part and a perfectly built claw. Real fingers need some give or a current limit so they do not crush or drop things.
  • No flex or backlash. Real links bend a little and real joints have play. The tool here is perfectly rigid.
  • Motor speeds are fixed numbers. In reality they change with load and battery voltage, and steppers can quietly skip steps if pushed too hard.
  • No collision checking. Nothing stops the arm from passing through the bin wall or folding into itself.
  • Open loop. Hobby motors do not report their real position, so the arm cannot tell when it has failed.

§10

Next steps

  • Model the arm properly in CAD and confirm the link lengths turn into parts that are actually stiff enough and reachable on a real desk.
  • Work out a torque budget for each joint and pick motors that can hold the poses, then order them.
  • Build and test one joint first, with a real load on the end, before committing to the whole arm.
  • Get the claw gripping a real object reliably.
  • Add the colour sorting with a camera once the mechanism itself is trustworthy.
  • Drive the physical arm straight from this page over a USB connection, using the same list of moves the simulation already produces.

§1

What this is

A 4-degree-of-freedom manipulator running entirely in the browser. You click the floor to place a part; the cell reads its world coordinates off the click, plans a waypoint sequence, and executes a pick-and-place into the bin. No physics engine, no robotics library — the kinematics and the gripper linkage are about a hundred and fifty lines of trigonometry.

It's written as the software half of a physical arm rather than as an animation. That constraint shaped most of the decisions below: a visual sim can cheat in ways that quietly become wrong the moment you wire up a motor.

§2

Kinematic model

The arm is a yaw joint at the base carrying a three-link chain. That decomposition is the whole trick. Any reachable point in 3D is a rotation about the vertical axis plus a position in the plane that rotation sweeps — so base yaw falls straight out of atan2(-z, x) in closed form, and the remaining three joints only ever solve a 2D problem in (r, height) where r = hypot(x, z).

The chain has four segments, not three. The gripper is a rigid 34 mm segment on the end of the wrist, so the solver targets the point between the fingertips rather than the wrist flange. This sounds pedantic and isn't: solve to the flange and every grasp is off by the length of the gripper. In simulation you'd never notice — the part snaps to the tool anyway. On hardware the arm drives its palm into the part and stalls.

§3

The inverse kinematics solver

Inverse kinematics is solved with cyclic coordinate descent. Starting at the wrist and working back to the shoulder, each joint rotates part of the way toward aligning the tool with the goal; the sweep repeats. Twelve passes per frame converges well inside a millimetre.

A three-link planar arm does have a closed-form solution, so CCD is a deliberate trade-off. It buys two things: joint limits become a one-line clamp() instead of case analysis across solution branches, and the same solver works unchanged if I add a link. It costs optimality — CCD converges to a configuration, not the best one, and it favours moving distal joints first, which is why the wrist does most of the work on small corrections.

§4

Why the base is a stepper

A full 360° workspace isn't a rendering choice, it's an actuator choice. A hobby servo sweeps 180° and stops, which would cut the reachable area to a half disc. So the base is a NEMA 17 stepper through a 4:1 belt: 200 full steps × 16 microsteps × 4 = 12,800 steps per revolution, or 35.6 steps per degree. That's the resolution the base actually commands, and the panel reports CH0 in steps rather than microseconds because steps are what the driver consumes.

Continuous rotation brings two problems a servo never has. The first is in software: yaw must take the short way around. Going from 179° to −179° is a 2° move, but naive subtraction says −358° and the arm spins almost all the way round to arrive at the same place. Every angle difference is wrapped into ±180° before it reaches the slew limiter.

The second is physical and this sim can't solve it for you: cables don't rotate forever. A genuinely continuous base needs a slip ring at the column, or the controller needs unwind logic that spends an idle moment turning the base back through 360° once it has accumulated too much twist. Ignore this and you will discover it by tearing out your own wiring harness.

§5

The gripper is a linkage, not an animation

The fingers pivot on hinge pins and are driven by a crank-slider: a linear actuator rod running down the palm, with a coupler link from the rod end to a pin on each finger. Push the rod, the couplers swing the fingers open; pull it, they close. One actuator, two symmetric jaws — the standard arrangement, and the reason grippers like this can be built from flat plate and three dowel pins.

The important part is that the rod position is solved, not faked. Given a finger angle θ, the coupler pin sits at a known point, and the rod must be wherever keeps the coupler exactly its own length: rodX = Px − √(Lc² − Pz²). So the mechanism on screen obeys the same constraint the aluminium would. Change couplerLength in CONFIG and the stroke changes correctly, including going stiff near the toggle point — which is exactly the feedback you want when picking linkage dimensions.

State is the pad-face gap in millimetres — what you'd measure with callipers around a part — and everything else derives from it: jaw angle by asin, rod travel by the constraint above, drive pulse from the rod travel. The fingers swing about 55°, which is finally visible. An earlier version slid two blocks 5 mm apart with no mechanism at all, which was both invisible and a lie about the hardware.

§6

Motion: rates, not durations

The first version gave each phase a fixed duration — "descend takes 0.8 s" — regardless of how far the arm had to travel. That's backwards from how a machine works and it bites immediately: a motor has a top speed, so a move needing 140° in 0.8 s from a joint that manages 90°/s puts the simulation and the bench on different trajectories from cycle one.

So durations are derived. Each waypoint declares a Cartesian feed rate — rapid, approach or precision, the same idea as G0 versus G1 — and time comes from path length ÷ feed rate. On top of that every axis is independently slew-limited to its own max rate. If a commanded path outruns the joints, the tool lags the goal and the FOLLOW ERR readout shows how far. That number is the same following error a real controller reports.

§7

Moves end on arrival, not on a clock

A waypoint completes when the tool is within the in-position tolerance and the jaws have reached their commanded gap — with a settle dwell after, and a timeout as a backstop. Advancing on a timer lets the sequence move on while the arm is still travelling. Every real motion controller has an in-position check for exactly this reason, and structuring the state machine around arrival rather than elapsed time is what lets the same waypoint list drive hardware.

§8

Drive output and program export

Joint angles in degrees are not what hardware consumes. The stepper base wants step counts; the servos want pulse widths; the gripper actuator wants pulses derived from rod travel, not from the pad gap, because the rod is the thing that physically moves. Each axis carries a calibration — range, direction flag for motors mounted backwards, trim offset for the fact that no horn ever lines up on the spline where you wanted. The panel shows live values per channel, which makes a mis-set direction obvious before you power the arm and hear it hit a hard stop.

EXPORT re-solves the planned cycle offline and downloads it as JSON: every waypoint with its Cartesian target, joint angles, jaw gap, jaw angle, rod travel, and the drive command per channel — plus the full calibration and kinematics used to generate it. That file is the actual handoff to an Arduino or ESP32. The browser is the teach pendant; the JSON is the program.

§9

Two bugs worth recording

Parts vanishing. An early version dropped parts using a bin counter only reset when the cell was cleared, so the release height climbed a pitch each cycle until parts were released above the bin walls and out of frame. The plan captured the counter at plan time while execution read it live, so the two disagreed mid-cycle.

Parts floating. Every part then went to the bin centre with random horizontal jitter, stacked one pitch higher each time — a one-dimensional tower with its blocks nudged sideways, visibly perched on nothing by the sixth part.

Same root cause both times: the resting position was invented in two places and checked against nothing. Now slotPosition(slot) is the single source of truth — parts pack a 3×3 grid per layer and only start a new layer once the one below is full — and each part carries an explicit FLOOR → HELD → BINNED state. Derived state recomputed in two places will eventually be recomputed two ways.

§10

What this still doesn't model

Worth being blunt, because these are the gaps between a green cycle here and a working arm on the bench:

  • No gravity or torque. The biggest one. A shoulder servo holding a 90 mm moment arm may stall or brown out under a load this sim carries for free. Budget torque before you trust a cycle.
  • No grasp force. Closing to part width assumes a rigid part and a perfectly calibrated linkage. Real fingers need compliance or a current limit.
  • No backlash. Belt reduction and hinge pins both have slop; the TCP here is exact.
  • Slew rates are constants. Real motor speed varies with load and supply voltage — a rate measured unloaded is optimistic. Steppers also lose steps silently when accelerated too hard.
  • No collision checking. The transfer path can pass through the bin walls, and nothing stops the arm folding into itself.
  • No cable model. See §4 — the 360° base assumes a slip ring you have to actually buy.
  • Open loop. Neither hobby servos nor bare steppers report position, so the arm can't know it failed. The follow error here is the sim's own, not feedback.

On top of that every axis is independently slew-limited to its own max rate. If a commanded path outruns the joints, the tool lags the goal and the FOLLOW ERR readout shows how far. That number is the same following error a real controller reports.

§7

Moves end on arrival, not on a clock

A waypoint completes when the tool is within the in-position tolerance and the jaws have reached their commanded gap — with a settle dwell after, and a timeout as a backstop. Advancing on a timer lets the sequence move on while the arm is still travelling. Every real motion controller has an in-position check for exactly this reason, and structuring the state machine around arrival rather than elapsed time is what lets the same waypoint list drive hardware.

01APPROACHrapidRise to clearance above the part, jaws open
02DESCENDprecisionStraight-line drop onto the part
03GRASP—Close jaws to part width; reparent to the tool
04LIFTapproachRaise to transit height
05TRANSFERrapidTraverse to above the assigned bin slot
06LOWERprecisionDescend onto the slot
07RELEASE—Open jaws; reparent to the world

§8

Drive output and program export

Joint angles in degrees are not what hardware consumes. The stepper base wants step counts; the servos want pulse widths; the gripper actuator wants pulses derived from rod travel, not from the pad gap, because the rod is the thing that physically moves. Each axis carries a calibration — range, direction flag for motors mounted backwards, trim offset for the fact that no horn ever lines up on the spline where you wanted. The panel shows live values per channel, which makes a mis-set direction obvious before you power the arm and hear it hit a hard stop.

EXPORT re-solves the planned cycle offline and downloads it as JSON: every waypoint with its Cartesian target, joint angles, jaw gap, jaw angle, rod travel, and the drive command per channel — plus the full calibration and kinematics used to generate it. That file is the actual handoff to an Arduino or ESP32. The browser is the teach pendant; the JSON is the program.

§9

Two bugs worth recording

Parts vanishing. An early version dropped parts using a bin counter only reset when the cell was cleared, so the release height climbed a pitch each cycle until parts were released above the bin walls and out of frame. The plan captured the counter at plan time while execution read it live, so the two disagreed mid-cycle.

Parts floating. Every part then went to the bin centre with random horizontal jitter, stacked one pitch higher each time — a one-dimensional tower with its blocks nudged sideways, visibly perched on nothing by the sixth part.

Same root cause both times: the resting position was invented in two places and checked against nothing. Now slotPosition(slot) is the single source of truth — parts pack a 3×3 grid per layer and only start a new layer once the one below is full — and each part carries an explicit FLOOR → HELD → BINNED state. Derived state recomputed in two places will eventually be recomputed two ways.

§10

What this still doesn't model

Worth being blunt, because these are the gaps between a green cycle here and a working arm on the bench:

  • No gravity or torque. The biggest one. A shoulder servo holding a 90 mm moment arm may stall or brown out under a load this sim carries for free. Budget torque before you trust a cycle.
  • No grasp force. Closing to part width assumes a rigid part and a perfectly calibrated linkage. Real fingers need compliance or a current limit.
  • No backlash. Belt reduction and hinge pins both have slop; the TCP here is exact.
  • Slew rates are constants. Real motor speed varies with load and supply voltage — a rate measured unloaded is optimistic. Steppers also lose steps silently when accelerated too hard.
  • No collision checking. The transfer path can pass through the bin walls, and nothing stops the arm folding into itself.
  • No cable model. See §4 — the 360° base assumes a slip ring you have to actually buy.
  • Open loop. Neither hobby servos nor bare steppers report position, so the arm can't know it failed. The follow error here is the sim's own, not feedback.
